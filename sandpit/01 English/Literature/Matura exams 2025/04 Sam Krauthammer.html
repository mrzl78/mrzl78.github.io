Characters

  • John: The son of the Director and Linda, grew up outside of the World State, spent his life al-ienated from his village on the New Mexico Savage Reservation, unable to fit in to World State society, entire worldview is based on his knowledge of Shakespeare’s plays.
  • Bernard Marx: An Alpha male who fails to fit in because of his inferior physical stature, holds unorthodox beliefs about sexual relationships, sports, and community events, insecure about his size and status, discontented with the World State, when threatened Bernard can be petty and cruel
  • Helmholtz Watson: An Alpha lecturer at the College of Emotional Engineering, prime example of his caste, but feels that his work is empty and meaningless and would like to use his writing abilities for something more meaningful. He and Bernard are friends because they find common ground in their discontent with the World State, but often finds Bernard’s boastfulness and cowardice tedious.
  • Lenina Crowne: A vaccination worker at the Central London Hatchery and Conditioning Centre, object of desire for a number of major and minor characters, including Bernard Marx and John, she is attracted to Bernard—the misfit—and she develops a violent passion for John the Savage. Ultimately, her values are those of a conventional World State citizen: her primary means of relating to other people is through sex, and she is unable to share Bernard’s disaffection or to comprehend John’s alternate system of values.
  • Mustapha Mond: The Resident World Controller of Western Europe, one of only ten World Con-trollers, once an ambitious, young scientist performing illicit research, given the choice of going into exile or training to become a World Controller, chose to give up science, and now he cen-sors scientific discoveries and exiles people for unorthodox beliefs.
  • Fanny Crowne: Lenina Crowne’s friend, role is mainly to voice the conventional values of her caste and society.
  • Henry Foster: One of Lenina’s many lovers, perfectly conventional Alpha male, casually discuss-ing Lenina’s body with his coworkers. His success with Lenina, and his casual attitude about it, infuriate the jealous Bernard.
  • Linda: John’s mother, and a Beta. While visiting the New Mexico Savage Reservation, she be-came pregnant with the Director’s son, got lost, suffered a head injury and was left behind, could not get an abortion on the Reservation, and she was too ashamed to return to the World State with a baby, makes her a social outcast, desperate to return to the World State and to soma.
  • The Director: The Director administrates the Central London Hatchery and Conditioning Centre. He is a threatening figure, with the power to exile Bernard to Iceland, secretly vulnerable be-cause he fathered a child, a scandalous and obscene act in the World State.
  • Popé: Popé was Linda’s lover on the New Mexico Savage Reservation. He gave Linda a copy of The Complete Works of Shakespeare.

Detailed Connections: (mistral)

The Dangers of Mass Hysteria and Control:
The Crucible: The play illustrates how mass hysteria and fear can lead to the destruction of a community. The witch trials in Salem are driven by paranoia and the abuse of power.
Brave New World: The novel depicts a society where control is maintained through technological advancements and psychological manipulation, leading to a loss of individuality and freedom.
Maus: The graphic novel shows the systematic dehumanization and control exerted by the Nazi regime during the Holocaust, leading to widespread suffering and trauma.

Abuse of Power and Manipulation of Truth:
The Crucible: The play highlights how those in power can manipulate the truth to serve their own interests, leading to injustice and suffering.
Brave New World: The novel explores how a totalitarian government can use science and technology to control and manipulate its citizens, suppressing dissent and individuality.
Maus: The graphic novel illustrates the abuse of power by the Nazi regime, which used propaganda and violence to manipulate and control the Jewish population.

Personal Integrity and Moral Courage:
The Crucible: Characters like John Proctor demonstrate the importance of personal integrity and moral courage in the face of oppression and injustice.
Brave New World: Characters like John the Savage struggle to maintain their individuality and moral values in a society that seeks to suppress them.
Maus: The graphic novel focuses on the personal stories of survivors, highlighting their resilience and moral courage in the face of unimaginable horrors.

Impact of Trauma and Historical Events:
The Crucible: The play explores the lasting impact of the witch trials on the community and individuals involved.
Brave New World: The novel examines the consequences of a society that has lost touch with its humanity and history.
Maus: The graphic novel delves into the intergenerational trauma experienced by Holocaust survivors and their families, emphasizing the importance of remembering and understanding historical events.
These connections highlight the universal themes of power, control, individuality, and the impact of historical events on society and individuals.
