Phoenician alphabet
The letters of the Phoenician alphabet, the earliest alphabetic system (end of 2nd millenium BC). Variations of this system spread into all kinds of regions and these simpler scripts were more widely used, if by an elite minority, though.

The Greeks, who called themselves Hellenes from the 8th century onwards, did not form a homogenous political nation. They consisted of several tribes and independent city-states (poleis, singular: polis). Nonetheless, the Greeks had a shared identity. They talked the same language, albeit in different dialects, and used the same alphabet inherited from the Phoenicians, a tradespeople from the eastern Mediterranean.

Mythology

Apart from that, the Greeks were unified by their religious beliefs. Greek religion was polytheistic, i.e., there were several gods. Although they were immortal and enjoyed eternal youth, they had strong human features and acted like mortals. According to the Greek belief system, the gods lived on Mount Olympus, the highest mountain in Greece, as a family. Their leaders were Zeus and his wife Hera. The Olympian gods (see Pantheon, below), all relatives of them, had different fields of duties: Apollo (healing, fortune-telling, poetry), Aphrodi­te (love), Artemis (hunt), Ares (war), Poseidon (the sea), Hermes (messenger of the gods, god of the travellers, merchants and thieves). In order to get the god’s help, they had to be appeased with sacrifices. These adoration ceremonies took place outside the temples. Some sanctuaries became very famous. In Olympia, Greeks from various states met since 776 BC to compete in sports in honour of Zeus. In Delphi, the Greeks consulted with the oracle of Apollo and also took part in games in his honour.

Arts

Art on pottery


Runners, c. 500 BC, Louvre

One of the characteristics of the visual arts was their uniformity throughout the numerous city-states. Until Hellenistic times, it was mostly communities who ordered pieces of art, so they were basically made for the public and for religious purposes (e.g. the Acropolis). Along vase painters, sculptors were very skilful. In classical times they managed to create vivid, lively sculptures which they integrated into buildings (temples, effigies, palaces). The first sculptural portraits were also made by Greek sculptors.

Literature, History

The literary and artistic achievements of the Greeks were a major influence for occidental culture. The two epics of the destruction of Troy (Iliad) and Ulysses’ Odyssey by Homer determined the Greeks’ picture of their gods and historical origins. Myths and sagas initially formed the major parts of Greek historiography, too. Then, Herodot (~485 until after 430) and Thukydides (~460 until ~400) introduced a new way of historical thinking. Both historians were keen on writing truthfully about contemporary events and on showing the roots of important events. Tragedy and comedy developed out of cultic performances in honour of the gods.

Philosophy

Initially, Greek philosophers were learned, wise men (e.g. Pythagoras, Thales), who tried to explain the world on a rational basis using different disciplines (e.g. ethics, mathematics, physics, music, poetry). Socrates (469 – 399) opposed the sophists, for whom man was the measure of all things. He looked for a base of the universe which was objective, outside human influence. He also looked for standards for human knowledge and for moral conducts. Plato (427 – 347) wrote Socrates’ teachings down in the form of dialogues. He himself thought about the ideal political state, which ought to be in harmony with human nature and the immortality of the human soul. Plato assumed that besides the ever-changing world, there are unchangeable archetypes (original forms) of all things, which man can only grasp through his thinking: ideas. Plato’s most influential student was Aristotle (384 – 322). He collected the whole knowledge of his time, and, by putting it into a clear, disctinct order, developed significant criteria for scientific work.
